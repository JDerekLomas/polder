Why Copilot & Claude Code stay blocked

Your best engineers and students can't touch the best AI tools — for good reasons.

✕ Code leaves the EU

US-hosted assistants send your source, research data and prompts to American servers. Under the US CLOUD Act and post-Schrems II scrutiny, that's a line legal and DPO offices won't cross.

✕ No contract they can sign

Public institutions can't approve an opaque per-seat US SaaS. There's no DPA on acceptable terms, no transparency, and procurement simply says no.

✕ Student & research data at risk

Anything touching personal or sensitive research data falls under GDPR. A black-box LLM in another jurisdiction is a non-starter for ethics boards.

✕ Sovereignty mandate

Dutch and EU policy is actively reducing dependence on US Big Tech and demanding public-values AI. Adopting a closed American agent runs against the institution's own strategy.

How it works

Three open layers. One sovereign stack.

Polder isn't a new model — it's a thin, transparent layer that assembles proven open components into something an institution can actually own, inspect and host.

  • Swap the backend in one line. Move from a hosted EU endpoint to your own GPUs without changing the workflow.
  • Inspect everything. Open source end to end — your security team can read the code that handles your code.
  • Stay current. A thin layer, not a hard fork — you ride upstream improvements instead of maintaining a clone.
